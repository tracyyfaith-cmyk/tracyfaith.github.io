Quality Standards

A disciplined framework for finance, research, and AI-evaluation work.

Each project is approached with explicit assumptions, traceable evidence, reproducible calculations, and a clear distinction between facts, estimates, model outputs, and professional judgment.

1. Define the financial decision

Clarify the question, audience, time horizon, output format, materiality threshold, and the practical decision the analysis must support.

  • Separate required facts from optional context.
  • Identify missing information and necessary assumptions.
  • Choose methods appropriate to the decision and available evidence.

2. Structure the reasoning

Break the work into source data, assumptions, calculations, accounting treatment, model logic, evaluation criteria, and conclusions.

  • Make dependencies between inputs and outputs explicit.
  • Prevent hidden assumptions from entering calculations.
  • Use consistent definitions, periods, units, and sign conventions.

3. Verify calculations independently

Recompute key outputs, reconcile financial statements, and test whether formulas correctly implement the stated method.

  • Check totals, ratios, percentages, and per-share values.
  • Trace tax, interest, depreciation, and working-capital effects.
  • Review circularity, timing mismatches, and denominator errors.

4. Test accounting consistency

Confirm that accounting treatments flow appropriately through income statements, cash-flow statements, balance sheets, and valuation outputs.

  • Review leases, revenue recognition, impairment, and stock compensation.
  • Assess NOLs, deferred taxes, and effective-tax-rate implications.
  • Distinguish operating, financing, and non-cash effects.

5. Challenge valuation assumptions

Evaluate whether forecasts, discount rates, terminal values, multiples, synergies, financing, and exit assumptions are coherent.

  • Compare base, upside, downside, and break-even cases.
  • Check consistency between growth, margins, reinvestment, and risk.
  • Assess whether conclusions remain valid under reasonable sensitivities.

6. Evaluate model outputs beyond fluency

A polished response is not automatically correct. Review factual accuracy, calculation quality, logical completeness, evidence support, and domain realism separately.

  • Identify subtle mistakes hidden behind confident wording.
  • Reject unsupported causal or market claims.
  • Distinguish acceptable simplification from material error.

7. Trace conclusions to evidence

Link conclusions to financial documents, model inputs, explicit calculations, experimental results, or clearly labeled assumptions.

  • Preserve source context and reporting period.
  • Separate retrieved evidence from generated interpretation.
  • Document uncertainty when evidence is incomplete or conflicting.

8. Document reproducibly

Record methodology, data preparation, model versions, evaluation rules, assumptions, limitations, and decision-relevant findings.

  • Use clear file, model, and experiment organization.
  • Provide sufficient detail for review and replication.
  • Communicate limitations without obscuring the final recommendation.

9. Calibrate task difficulty

For AI training and evaluation, ensure tasks require real finance reasoning rather than simple label recognition, direct copying, or trivial arithmetic.

  • Use realistic assumptions and financially meaningful distractors.
  • Require integration of multiple facts, statements, or calculations.
  • Keep the correct answer objective, defensible, and independently verifiable.

10. Deliver decision-ready communication

Present the answer in a structure appropriate for analysts, executives, investment committees, researchers, or model reviewers.

  • Lead with the conclusion when the context requires it.
  • Explain the key drivers and material risks.
  • Use tables, models, and narrative only where each adds value.
